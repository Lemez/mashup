Look at this sentence:

The cat, "who had just got up", brushed her teeth with a fishbone.

The underlined part is called a relative clause - it's something in the middle of a sentence that adds more meaning.

A relative clause always starts with a relative pronoun - a word like that, who, where and which - that relates to the word before. Here we need a comma to start it, and a comma to finish with.

The tortoise that comes here on Tuesdays is wearing glasses.

In this sentence, the relative clause is so important that we don't have to put any commas around it. The rule is: if you take out the relative clause and you don't know who we are talking about, you can leave the commas out.
